Research

I enjoy applying knowledge informed probabilistic reasoning (mainly Bayesian Reasoning) and Machine Learning techniques to solve challenging problems in sustainability and healthcare. My research interests include Semantic Web, Information Extraction, Internet of Things (IoT), and knowledge representation and reasoning under uncertainty.

Specifically, I work on algorithms that can leverage existing knowledge of a domain (e.g., ConceptNet, domain ontology, dictionaries) to complement structure and parameters of probabilistic models. My thesis work involves extracting events, understanding events, and recommending actions using probabilistic models in domains such as Smart Cities and Healthcare.

    Smart Cities

    My work is focused on extracting and understanding city related events from short textual observations such as SMS (Short Message Service) and tweets. Among textual observation sources, twitter has been a valuable source of information providing an unbiased bottom-up view of city's infrastructure and it's inhabitants. I try to answer these questions in my research by utilizing both sensor data and textual streams from citizens of a city :

  • Are textual observations a feasible source for extracting city events?
  • How do textual observations complement other modalities such as sensor observations?
  • How can we extract city events from textual sources?
  • How can we understand the relationships between various city events?

    • Event Extraction: I'm investigating on city infrastructure related event extraction techniques from informal text such as tweets. Tweets are a good source of city related events but are often messy and offers many challenges in processing them. Informal nature, use of slangs, short forms, redundancy, and noisy nature are some of the challenges offered during event extraction. I'm using techniques such as sequence labeling (e.g., Conditional Random Fields) and annotation using domain knowledge (e.g., Open Street Maps, 511.org). The event extration architecture is shown here. In the past, I have conducted research on event extraction from SMS messages. I worked with Dr. Biplav Srivastava of IBM Research, India during my internship in 2012 for delay estimation of public transport vehicles using events from SMS messages. My research was integrated into an internal product for journey planning. This work also led to the filing of two disclosures and received an award for best research show case (along with two other entries) among 40 other intern participants.

    Event Understanding: This step facilitates extraction of knowledge about interactions between various events extrated in the previous step. City events exhibit intricate interactions for example, events such as sporting events and music events may result in slow moving traffic. Bad weather such as poor visibility may result in slow moving traffic. For understanding such interactions, I have investigated on Bayesian Network (BN) structure extraction algorithms. I have proposed to complement BN structure extraction with declarative domain knoweldge to assign edge directions. I'm currently investigating on utilizing temporal dynamics in understanding speed variations. When we learn normalcy using the temporal dynamics model, we seek explanations from other sources such as textual observations when the speed dynamics is abnormal.

    Action Recommendation: After understanding city events and its impact, I have investigated its effect on the schedule of public transport vehicles. Meta data of traffic related events extracted from textual messages were utilized for assessing the extent of the event affecting public transport routes. A event aware recommendation of route was provided to the users seeking public transport from a source to destination in a city.

    Preventive and Personalized Health Care


    Observations concerning our health and well-being spans Physical, Cyber, and Social systems . Further, increasing trend in the availability of low-cost sensors for recording physiological observations provide unprecedented opportunities to extract and understand health signals. Most of the existing research in understanding a disease utilize multi-sensory observations from a single modality. Diseases such as Asthma pose unique challenges due to the high degree of symptomatic variability and individual's response to allergens. Further, health observations for Asthma span Physical (activity, coughing), Cyber (alerts of pollen), and Social (incidents of asthma attacks) worlds. My work is geared toward answering the following questions:

  • How can we provide decision support to doctors by collecting asthma related observations?
  • How can we extract health signals from textual streams?
  • How can we understand the relationships between various health signals?
  • Can we use this understanding to build an asthma early-warning system?

    LOKILAS: Lost Key Identification, Localization, and Alerting System

    This project aims to solve a practical problem encountered by researchers in some of the Kno.e.sis labs regarding their shared lab keys. The keys in each lab are a shared resource to give the researchers access to shared facilities, such as conference rooms. Unfortunately, people who use the keys too often forget to return the keys in a timely manner and there is currently no system in place to track said keys. This can cause inconvenience and frustration for individuals who later need to use a facility but are unable to find the required key. Currently, the solution to this problem is to attach larger objects to a shared key, such as the key shown in Photo 1, so that the key cannot be forgotten in a pocket. While this solution may work, there is still an inconvenience to carrying the larger keychain. Project LOKILAS hopes to create an intuitive system for tracking and recovering the shared keys.

    Trust in Sensor and Social Networks

    Trust relationships occur naturally in many diverse contexts such as ecommerce, social interactions, social networks, ad hoc mobile networks, distributed systems, decision-support systems, (semantic) sensor web, emergency response scenarios, etc. As the connections and interactions between humans and/or machines (collectively called agents) evolve, and as the agents providing content and services become increasingly removed from the agents that consume them, miscreants attempt to corrupt, subvert or attack existing infrastructure. This in turn calls for support for robust trust inference (e.g., gleaning, aggregation, propagation) and update (also called trust management). Unfortunately, there is neither a universal notion of trust that is applicable to all domains nor a clear explication of its semantics in many situations. Because Web, social networking and sensor information often provide complementary and overlapping information about an activity or event that are critical for overall situational awareness, there is a unique need for understanding and development of techniques for managing trust that span all these information channels. Currently, we are pursuing research on trust and trustworthiness issues in interpersonal, social, and sensor networks, to potentially unify and integrate them for exploiting their complementary strengths.
